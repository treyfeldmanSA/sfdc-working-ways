The "Definition of Ready" is our team's shared quality checklist. It's a simple agreement that a story must have clear requirements, acceptance criteria, and necessary designs before it can be pulled into a sprint. This is our primary tool for preventing chaos. It stops half-baked ideas from disrupting our focus and ensures that when a developer picks up a task, they have everything they need to start work immediately.

If a story is too large to be completed in a single sprint, it's a sign that it needs to be broken down into smaller, independent chunks of value. The job of the tech leads and product manager is to slice these "epics" into sprint-sized stories before they are ever considered for planning. This ensures we can still deliver value incrementally, even on large projects.

If you're used to 30+ minute standups, 15 minutes might feel shockingly short. Here's the difference: we're not trying to solve problems in standup anymore. No detailed technical explanations, no debugging sessions, no "well actually..." discussions. Planning Poker

Planning Poker is a collaborative estimation technique where team members use cards (typically numbered 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21) to vote on the complexity or effort required for a story.

How it works: Everyone privately selects a card representing their estimate, then all cards are revealed simultaneously. If there's disagreement (like a 3 and a 13), those people explain their reasoning. The team discusses and revotes until consensus is reached.

Why it's effective: It prevents anchoring bias (where the first person's estimate influences everyone else), encourages discussion about hidden complexity, and ensures the whole team understands the work involved.
