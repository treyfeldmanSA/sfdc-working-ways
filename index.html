Frequently Asked Questions

Let's address some common concerns upfront.

This is a strategic choice to improve work-life balance and focus. A mid-week cadence avoids the "Friday rush" to finish work and prevents stakeholders from missing important demos. It turns Friday into a productive "maker day" to start new work, and ensures we don't lose context over the weekend before a big planning session on Monday.

Our goal is to deploy at the end of every sprint. Shipping value frequently is key to reducing risk and increasing momentum. Each deployment will be small and manageable. If our process makes this difficult, then improving our deployment automation will become a priority task within our sprints.

It's a common concern, but teams almost always find the opposite is true. We'll complete fewer story points in a single two-week sprint than a four-week one, but our overall throughput of valuable, completed work will increase. This is because shorter sprints dramatically reduce wasted time from context-switching, working on the wrong things due to delayed feedback, and having work sit idle waiting for review. We're shifting our focus from being "busy" to being "effective."

This is exactly what the Backlog Refinement meeting is for. If a story is too large to be completed in a single sprint, it's a sign that it needs to be broken down into smaller, independent chunks of value. The job of the tech leads and product manager is to slice these "epics" into sprint-sized stories before they are ever considered for planning. This ensures we can still deliver value incrementally, even on large projects.

The "Definition of Ready" is our team's shared quality checklist. It's a simple agreement that a story must have clear requirements, acceptance criteria, and necessary designs before it can be pulled into a sprint. This is our primary tool for preventing chaos. It stops half-baked ideas from disrupting our focus and ensures that when a developer picks up a task, they have everything they need to start work immediately.

We will plan for it honestly. As part of our roadmap, we will formally allocate a 20% capacity buffer in every sprint dedicated to handling urgent non-roadmap work. This means we won't constantly be "interrupting" planned work; we'll be using a pre-allocated budget for it. This makes our planning more realistic and protects our roadmap commitments.
